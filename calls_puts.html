Module 01

The four positions
every trader must know

Every option trade is one of four things: buying a call, selling a call, buying a put, or selling a put. Understand these four shapes and every strategy in the world is just a combination of them.

Rights vs obligations

Options give rights to buyers and impose obligations on sellers. The asymmetry is the whole point — the buyer pays a premium for protection from downside, while the seller collects that premium in exchange for bearing the risk.

The buyer's loss is always capped at the premium paid. The seller's maximum profit is always the premium collected. These limits are what makes options so versatile — you can define your exact worst-case scenario before entering any trade.
Buy Call

Long call

You pay a premium for the right to buy 100 shares at the strike price on or before expiry. If the stock rises above the strike by more than the premium, you profit. If it doesn't, you lose only the premium — nothing more.

Who buys calls?
Bullish traders who want leveraged upside with limited downside. The premium is the maximum you can lose regardless of how far the stock falls.
Best when: you expect a significant rally and want to control more shares with less capital than buying the stock outright.
The math
Profit = Stock price − Strike − Premium (if exercised)

Max loss = Premium paid
Breakeven = Strike + Premium
Max gain = Unlimited

Watch out for theta decay — time value erodes daily. A long call that's right about direction but too slow will still lose money. Give yourself enough time.
Sell Call

Short call

You collect premium immediately. In return, you are obligated to sell shares at the strike if the buyer exercises. Maximum profit is the premium collected. If the stock rises sharply, losses are theoretically unlimited.

Who sells calls?
Income-focused traders who believe the stock will stay below the strike. Often done "covered" — against shares already owned — to reduce risk.
Naked short calls are extremely high risk. One unexpected rally can erase months of premium income.
The math
Profit = Premium − max(0, Stock − Strike)

Max profit = Premium collected
Breakeven = Strike + Premium
Max loss = Unlimited

Buy Put

Long put

You pay a premium for the right to sell shares at the strike price. If the stock falls below the strike by more than the premium, you profit. Maximum loss is the premium. Maximum profit is the strike minus premium (stock can only reach zero).

Who buys puts?
Bearish traders and investors hedging a stock portfolio. A put on your stock position works exactly like insurance — you pay a premium to cap your downside.
Best when: you expect a significant decline OR want to protect a stock position from a crash without selling the shares.
The math
Profit = Strike − Stock price − Premium (if exercised)

Max loss = Premium paid
Breakeven = Strike − Premium
Max gain = Strike − Premium (stock → 0)

Unlike short-selling a stock, buying a put limits your risk to the premium paid — even if the stock goes to $1,000. That defined risk makes long puts safer than being short the shares directly.
Sell Put

Short put

You collect premium immediately. In return, you are obligated to buy shares at the strike if the buyer exercises. Maximum profit is the premium. If the stock falls sharply, you must buy at the strike — potentially far above market price.

Who sells puts?
Income-seeking traders who are bullish or neutral, and happy to own the stock at the strike price. "Cash-secured" means holding the cash to buy the stock if assigned.
Classic use: "I'd buy AAPL at $150 anyway — so I'll sell a $150 put and get paid to wait for that price."
The math
Profit = Premium − max(0, Strike − Stock)

Max profit = Premium collected
Breakeven = Strike − Premium
Max loss = Strike − Premium (stock → 0)
